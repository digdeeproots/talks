# Core Insight — The Gift

*Captured from conversation 2026-05-11. Raw, cleaned for readability, not reorganized.*

---

## The Toil Reality

The vast majority of every week's work is toil — stuff that anyone could do, perhaps at a slightly different rate or quality level, but just stuff. The whole point of AI for coding is to do the toil. That's why consistency matters: we can't swap the toil of doing work for vigilance-toil. Vigilance is just different toil, and more stressful. We need to actually delegate the toil.

## The Hour

Every developer does about 30-60 minutes of uniquely valuable work per day. Only an hour a week actually leverages their perspective — something that can't be regurgitated by a machine spitting out the Best Practice answer. The rest is waste — the toil that gets in the way. This is currently hidden. Everyone sees the 50 hours of work and assumes that's what developers do.

## The Crossroads

Companies don't need 40x output. They can't keep up with the dev rate they already have — otherwise they'd hire more developers. They have the money, not the need.

So what happens at the crossroads?

- **Option A: Layoffs.** 39 of every 40 developers let go. Gives shareholders a little extra cash, but not much — software is hugely profitable anyway. And companies lose their ability to see perspectives, to ground. That costs competitively in the medium and long term. Many will go this way. Enough to create strife, labor disputes, uprisings.
- **Option B: Massive vacations.** People stay sharp for the one hour they're called on. Probably not viable at scale.
- **Option C: Something else.** Workers who actually have the power — because they are the ones who actually create — find newer, better ways to work together.

## The Grounding Insight

Each developer has unique value: their perspective, their different grounding in a different reality, their ability to wrangle alignment between different groups. That's what's worth leveraging. Perhaps developers each work at half a dozen companies, 2 hours a week at each, providing grounding and wrangling with different problems. That creates a powerful ground that can stabilize thousands of toil-executing AIs — especially once AI noise (inconsistencies and errors that require vigilance) has been eliminated.

## The Bigger Question

The question isn't how roles change. It's how identity shifts — human identity, corporate identity — and how we find different ways to cooperate. How we engineer not only the ecosystem our agents can see, but the ecosystem we can see, the ecosystem our companies can see. How we see a different world, with different tools, optimizing toward a different, more collaborative outcome.

Companies that do the layoffs gain short-term advantage and lose their ability to ground and turn. Companies that find the newer paths can turn quickly, adapt readily, run larger pools of agents without losing direction. They explore many directions at once and choose whichever wins. They have true abundance.

## The Call

Work together. Take small actions. Start engineering the worlds our minions live in, and the worlds we ourselves live in. With consistency, with safety, designed for carelessness.

**Designed for careless abundance.**

## What the Talk Is Actually For

Find the other world builders. People who can align, and who can bring others into alignment. Each in a different walk of life, a different company or government, a different role.

This is the audience within the audience. The talk's hidden purpose is recognition — help those people see themselves, then find each other.

## Why It Must Stay Grounded

Many people have spoken the vision above. Hippies and hipsters have beaten those drums for decades and been sidelined. The difference is practicality. This talk must focus on solving a hard, real problem — and then show that we are living inside the same system, and that we can change it.

The grounding: things like safeguarding have been doing exactly this for decades. Decider protocol has always existed, yet most decisions got made by authority — because facilitation was hard, few knew the protocol, and one person was always willing to take power. But now you can build a simple tool that takes ownership of the decision-making process and delegates power to speak, to express a position, to vote at the right moment. It shows you how to participate in consent-based democracy. The tool makes the superior technique practical.

Same thing: a RAG over every decision and conversation a company has ever had. Chat with it. Group memory becomes accessible. The tool shapes reality.

## The Fractal

The same principle — design the world to make right behavior the easy behavior — applies at every level:

- **Agent level**: design the environment your AI lives in (what it perceives, what decisions it faces, what it can't do by accident)
- **Human level**: design the tools you use to decide, remember, reflect, understand another's perspective
- **Group level**: design how you align across groups, how you find groups you don't align with, how you establish the terms of interaction

Engineer your agents' environments — design for carelessness, for maliciousness, for unaligned and immoral behavior. Then look at what tools *you* use to decide, to remember, to reflect, to understand someone else's perspective, to align across groups, to find groups with whom you don't align, and to establish how you interact with them.

The tool shapes reality. At every scale.

## The Call (revised)

Not "do this technique." Not "implement this system." The call is: recognize yourself in this. Then find others.

## The Gift (distilled)

Most of what we call developer work is toil. AI can take the toil — but only if you design your AI system so you don't have to watch it constantly. Designing for carelessness is what that means. When you pull it off, you get back the hour that was always the real work. And then the real question begins.
