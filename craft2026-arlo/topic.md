## Abstract (draft)

Most of what we call developer work is toil. Not creative work, not judgment, not the application of hard-won perspective — just toil. Consistent, repeatable, draining. And AI can do toil.

But there's a trap. Most teams swapping work-toil for vigilance-toil — watching their AI closely enough that nothing goes wrong. That's just different toil, and more stressful. The promise of AI isn't a new kind of toil. It's getting back the hour a day that was always the real work.

The way out is a design principle with a counterintuitive name: design for carelessness. When someone makes a mistake, the wrong response is "be more careful." The right question is: what could we change about the system so that someone being even *more* careless than we were would still succeed? That's not a new idea. Safeguarding has applied it to software development for decades — using your defect stream to eliminate the hazards that make mistakes likely, not to punish the people who made them.

Now apply the same principle to your AI agents. Design the world they live in so careless behavior succeeds. What can they see? What choices do they face? Which decisions have you moved to deterministic code so the agent never has to make them at all?

And then apply it to how your team makes decisions. Consent-based protocols that let a thousand people explore a thousand directions — not in cacophony, but in harmony.

This is one principle at three scales. It works. We have examples. And it opens a question worth spending the rest of your career on.

*Come ready to explore the model — and to meet the other people in the room who are already building.*
